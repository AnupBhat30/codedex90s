Feynman’s Philosophy

Feynman’s secret lay in understanding the true essence of a concept rather than merely knowing its name, leading to his remarkable achievements. This learning technique can be applied universally, irrespective of the subject.

"The person who says he knows what he thinks but cannot express it usually does not know what he thinks." - Mortimer Adler

Step 1: Select a Concept and Map Your Knowledge

Start with a blank page. Write everything you know about your chosen topic, using a different color pen for new information as you learn. This creates a visual map of your growing understanding.

Step 2: Explain It to a 12-Year-Old

Once you feel confident you understand it, try to explain the concept using only simple words a child would understand. You haven’t grasped it fully if you can’t explain it simply.

"Complex language often masks gaps in knowledge."

"Anyone can make a subject complicated but only someone who understands can make it simple."

Step 3: Review and Refine

Write out your explanation, then read it aloud. Clear writing reveals gaps in understanding and gives poor thinking nowhere to hide.

Look for:

  • Confusing explanations
  • Hidden jargon
  • Glossed-over concepts

When you find weak spots, return to the source material. Study those sections until you can explain them simply. Keep refining until a 12-year-old would understand every part.

Step 4: Test and Archive

Test your understanding by teaching someone else. Note their questions and points of confusion – these reveal where your explanation needs work. Once satisfied, archive your simple explanation in a learning binder for periodic review.

Conclusion

"Simplicity reveals mastery. You’ve achieved true understanding when you can strip away jargon and explain complex ideas simply."
